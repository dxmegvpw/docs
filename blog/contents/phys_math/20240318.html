Why the dimension of representation of gamma matrices \(\gamma^{\mu}\) is even?

I will prove why the odd-dimensional representation of the gamma matrices is prohibited. You already have almost the answer to this problem, they are traceless. Let us review its property.

The gamma matrices are defined by the relation \[ \{\gamma^{\mu},\gamma^{\nu}\}=2g^{\mu\nu}\cdot\mathrm{\textbf{1}}_{4} \] and it implies the matrices anticommuting with each other. Thus we take the trace for the product of two different matrices \(\gamma^{\mu}, \gamma^{\nu}\) like \[ \mathrm{tr}[\gamma^{\nu}\gamma^{\mu}\gamma^{\nu}] \quad (\text{not summed for indices $\mu$}). \] Evaluating this form by using the cyclicity of the trace, we find \( +\text{tr}\gamma^{\mu}=-\text{tr}\gamma^{\mu} \) and then conclude they are traceless. And you also get the condition that the eigenvalue of \(\gamma^{0}\) are only \(\pm 1\) since it satisfies \((\gamma^{0})^2=1\).

You assumed \(\gamma^{0}\) is diagonalized as \[ \gamma^{0} = \begin{pmatrix} c_{1} & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & c_{2} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & c_{3} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & c_{N} \end{pmatrix} \] where \(c_{i}=\pm 1\) for every \(i=1,\cdots N\) since they are eigenvalue of \(\gamma^{0}\). From the above hypothesis, we can prove that \(N\) is an even number and the dimension of the representations is also even.
